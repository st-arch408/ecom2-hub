# QXO TURNS EVERY JOB SITE ADDRESS INTO A SELF-CONTAINED BUSINESS COMMAND CENTER

**New Job Management Hub Gives Contractors a Single Digital Record That Connects Every Estimate, Order, Delivery, and Dollar — From First Lead to Final Invoice — Without Paying for a CRM**

**Seattle, WA — 2026** — Marcus Rivera was sitting at his kitchen table on a Sunday night doing the thing he hated most about running his own roofing business: trying to figure out what was happening across twelve active jobs.

He had a whiteboard in his garage with addresses and install dates scrawled in dry-erase marker. He had a text thread with his branch rep about a shingle color backorder on the Henderson job. He had a spreadsheet he hadn't updated in two weeks with estimated margins that were already wrong. He had a sticky note on his dashboard reminding him to follow up with the homeowner on Elm Street who hadn't responded to his proposal in nine days. And he had a voicemail from his accountant asking why three invoices from last month didn't match the materials he'd actually ordered.

Marcus wasn't failing because he couldn't install roofs. He was drowning because every job he touched existed in a different place — a text message here, a branch receipt there, a proposal PDF buried in his email somewhere — and none of it talked to each other. The more jobs he took on, the more time he spent managing the chaos instead of doing the work.

"I realized I was spending my Sundays not resting, not planning, but just trying to reconstruct what happened during the week," Marcus said. "Which jobs did I send proposals on? Which ones got accepted? Did the materials show up at the right site? What did I actually make on the Thompson job after that return? I'd piece it together from texts, emails, receipts, and my memory. Every week, something fell through the cracks. A follow-up I forgot. A delivery I didn't track. Margin I couldn't account for."

Marcus looked at CRM software. AccuLynx wanted $200 a month. ServiceTitan started at $300. JobNimbus was somewhere in between. For a solo operator clearing maybe $8,000 a month in take-home after materials, labor, insurance, and truck payments, that's real money — especially for software that doesn't connect to where he actually buys his materials. He'd still be entering the same data twice: once in the CRM, once on his supplier's website. The CRM would track his pipeline. His supplier would track his orders. And he'd be the human bridge between them, which is exactly the problem he was trying to solve.

Today, Marcus doesn't use a CRM. He doesn't need one.

Every address Marcus touches on QXO is now a Job — a single, persistent digital record that holds everything about that project in one place. The estimate he built through the ATB Blue calculator. The good-better-best proposal he sent the homeowner. The e-signature when they accepted the mid-tier package. The automatic Premium measurement upgrade. The materials order that fired to his branch without a phone call. The delivery tracking with GPS and proof-of-delivery photos. The invoice. The realized margin — what he charged versus what he spent, calculated automatically from data that already lived in the system.

Marcus doesn't manage twelve jobs across twelve places anymore. He opens QXO on his phone and sees twelve Jobs — each one a complete story from lead to close, organized by address, updated in real time, and connected to every order, delivery, and dollar that flowed through it.

"The thing that changed isn't that I got a new app," Marcus said. "It's that I stopped losing information. Every address is its own little universe now. I tap on 445 Spruce Way and I can see the proposal I sent, when they signed, what materials shipped, what I charged, what I actually spent. I used to guess at my margins. Now I just look. And when a homeowner on Elm Street calls me back three months later wanting a siding quote, that address already knows who they are, what I've done there before, and what's pending. I don't have to dig through my texts to remember."

The shift is structural. Jobs is not a feature bolted onto the existing QXO ecommerce experience. It is the organizing principle of Ecom 2.0 — the backbone that everything else connects to. ATB Blue creates Jobs. The Build button creates Jobs. Branch-initiated orders create Jobs. Every order placed through QXO, whether a contractor taps it out on their phone or calls their branch and an inside sales rep enters it at the counter, gets associated with a Job tied to a property address. There are no orphaned orders in the system. There are no loose receipts floating outside of a project context. Every transaction has a home.

For contractors like Marcus — Tier 3 solo operators and small crews who represent roughly 60% of QXO's customer base — Jobs delivers roughly 80% of the value they'd get from an enterprise CRM, for free. Not a trial. Not a freemium tier with crippled features. Free, because it's built on top of the supply chain data that QXO already owns. The estimates, orders, deliveries, and invoices are QXO transactions. Organizing them into a coherent project record doesn't require a separate subscription — it requires a platform that was designed to think in jobs instead of shopping carts.

"The roofing industry has a software problem disguised as a workflow problem," said Brad Jacobs, CEO at QXO. "Contractors don't need another tool to buy. They need a platform that knows what they're working on and keeps the whole picture in one place. A CRM that isn't connected to your supply chain is just a prettier spreadsheet. Jobs gives contractors the project visibility they've been paying hundreds of dollars a month for — or more often, going without — and it's built into QXO because the data is already here. We're not asking contractors to enter their orders into a CRM. We're showing them their orders already organized into jobs."

Sarah Chen runs Peak Ridge Roofing with a four-person crew. Before Jobs, she tracked her pipeline on a yellow legal pad and reconciled her margins once a quarter in a panic before her accountant filed taxes. "I'd look at my bank account and know I was making money, but I couldn't tell you which jobs were profitable and which ones I was losing my shirt on," she said. "Now every job has its own P&L, basically. I can see that the Lakewood tearoff made me 38% but the Aurora hip-and-valley only cleared 22% because I underestimated the accessories. That kind of visibility used to require software I couldn't afford. Now it's just... there."

Sarah also uses Jobs to manage cross-trade work. When the homeowner at one of her roofing addresses asked about siding, the Job was already open. She added a siding scope to the same property — a separate section within the same address record, with its own estimate, its own materials, its own margin tracking. The address holds everything. Each trade is its own self-contained section, like different sections of an orchestra. Sarah is the conductor. The Job is the score.

QXO Jobs is available to all QXO contractors as part of the Ecom 2.0 platform. There is no subscription fee, no per-seat charge, and no premium tier to unlock. Jobs is free because it's the backbone of how QXO works — and the more contractors use it, the better the platform gets for everyone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a Job, and how is it different from an order?

An order is a transaction. A Job is the full story.

On QXO today, a contractor places an order and that order exists as a standalone record — a receipt with a date, a list of materials, a price, and a delivery status. If the contractor placed an estimate first, that estimate lives in a separate system. If they sent a proposal to a homeowner, that proposal lives in their email. If materials were delivered, the proof-of-delivery photo lives somewhere else. If the contractor wants to know what they actually made on the project, they have to reconstruct it manually from receipts, invoices, and memory.

A Job collapses all of that into a single, persistent record anchored to a property address. The Job for 445 Spruce Way contains the ATB Blue estimate, the three-tier proposal, the homeowner's e-signature, the automatic measurement upgrade, the materials order, the delivery confirmation with GPS tracking and site photos, the invoice, and the calculated margin — estimated versus actual. Every data point that touches that address lives inside the Job. Nothing is orphaned. Nothing requires the contractor to be the connective tissue between disconnected systems.

The data model is straightforward: a Job is an address with structured children. Estimates, orders, deliveries, invoices, margin data, notes, and accounting references all attach to the Job as nested records. When a contractor opens a Job, they see the complete lifecycle of that project in chronological order — not a pile of unrelated transactions.

The critical distinction: an order is something a contractor places and forgets. A Job is something they manage and return to. An order ends when the delivery arrives. A Job ends when the work is done, the margin is calculated, and the homeowner is a reference for future business. And even then, the Job persists — because if that homeowner calls back in six months for another scope of work, the address already has context.

### How does Jobs connect to ATB Blue and the Build button?

Jobs is the backbone. ATB Blue and Build are the front doors.

When a contractor hits the Build button and selects New Estimate, the system creates a Job for that address. The ATB Blue flow — $20 Bid Perfect measurement, good-better-best proposal generation, homeowner delivery, automated order on acceptance — all happens inside that Job. The estimate, the proposal, the acceptance, the measurement upgrade, and the materials order are all nested records within a single Job tied to that property address.

When a contractor selects New Order through Build for a job they've already won, the system either creates a new Job or attaches the order to an existing one at that address. When they select From Template or Reorder from Job, the same logic applies — every Build path creates or attaches to a Job.

The relationship flows in one direction: ATB Blue and Build are ingress points — they are how work enters the system. Jobs is where that work lives once it arrives. A contractor doesn't "go to Jobs" to start working. They hit Build, do their thing, and the Job is created and populated as a natural consequence of the workflow. Jobs becomes the place they return to — to check a delivery status, review a margin, follow up with a homeowner, or start a new scope at the same address.

This distinction matters because it avoids the CRM trap of making contractors organize their work before they do their work. Nobody wants to "create a project" before they can send a quote. In Ecom 2.0, the project creates itself the moment the contractor starts working. The Job is an output of the workflow, not an input to it.

### What can a contractor see and do inside a Job?

A Job is the contractor's single source of truth for a property. Everything they need to know about an active or completed project is organized in one place, accessible from their phone or laptop.

When a contractor opens a Job, they see the full timeline: when the lead came in, when the estimate was created, which proposal tiers were generated, when the proposal was sent, whether the homeowner accepted (and which tier), when the materials order was placed, real-time delivery tracking, proof-of-delivery photos, the invoice, and the realized margin.

The specific capabilities within a Job include:

Proposal tracking and status — see every proposal sent from that address, whether it's pending, accepted, declined, or expired, and which tier the homeowner selected. If the homeowner declined, the structured reason is captured so the contractor can learn from it over time.

Order history and materials detail — every order associated with that address, whether it originated from an ATB Blue acceptance, a direct Build order, a template, or a branch-initiated purchase. Materials, quantities, pricing at the time of order, and fulfillment status are all visible.

Delivery tracking — real-time GPS tracking of delivery trucks, scheduled delivery windows, proof-of-delivery photos uploaded by the driver, and digital invoice confirmation pushed automatically into the Job record.

Margin analysis — estimated margin (what the contractor quoted the homeowner) versus realized margin (what they actually spent on materials through QXO). This is calculated automatically from the proposal data and the order/invoice data — the contractor doesn't enter anything manually. Over time, this data teaches the system how accurately the contractor estimates, which feeds back into the Builds intelligence layer to improve future defaults.

Notes and context — a space for the contractor to add their own notes about the property, the homeowner, the scope of work, or anything else they want to remember. This replaces the text messages, sticky notes, and mental bookmarks that contractors currently rely on.

The design principle is digital-centric. A contractor should be able to open their phone or laptop and have all the information they need about any active or completed project — equal to or better than what they'd get by calling the branch or driving there in person. The branch visit becomes optional, not required. The phone call becomes a choice, not a necessity.

### How does Jobs handle branch-initiated orders and non-digital work?

Every order placed through QXO — whether the contractor entered it on their phone, placed it through the Build button, or walked into a branch and had a counter rep ring it up — gets associated with a Job.

This is a deliberate architectural decision. The system does not distinguish between "digital orders" and "branch orders" at the Job level. If a contractor calls their inside sales rep and says "I need 30 squares of Timberline HDZ delivered to 445 Spruce Way on Thursday," the ISR enters that order into the system and it attaches to the Job for 445 Spruce Way. If a Job doesn't exist yet for that address, one is created automatically.

This matters because contractors don't make a clean choice between digital and branch. A contractor might generate a proposal on their phone through ATB Blue, then call the branch to add a specialty item the next day, then reorder underlayment through the app when they realize they're short on-site. All three transactions attach to the same Job. The contractor sees one unified record, not three disconnected orders.

The intent is that Jobs becomes the connective tissue that makes the branch relationship stronger, not redundant. Branch reps can see a contractor's Jobs the same way the contractor does — which means when a contractor calls to ask about a delivery, the rep already has full context. No more "let me pull up that order, what was the PO number?" The rep opens the Job for 445 Spruce Way and sees everything.

For contractors who do most of their purchasing through the branch — the Tier 2 mid-market crews with established rep relationships — Jobs provides digital visibility into work they've always done offline. They don't have to change how they buy. They just gain a digital record of what they bought, organized by project instead of buried in a transaction log.

### Why is Jobs free?

Because Jobs is made of data QXO already has.

Every estimate, order, delivery, and invoice that flows through QXO is a QXO transaction. The data exists. It lives in QXO's systems. Today, that data is organized by transaction — an order here, a delivery there, an invoice somewhere else. Jobs reorganizes that same data by address and project. It doesn't require the contractor to enter new information. It doesn't pull data from external systems that QXO would need to license. It connects dots that already exist inside QXO's own infrastructure.

A standalone CRM charges $200-500 per month because it has to build and maintain the entire data layer from scratch. The contractor manually enters every lead, every proposal, every order, every invoice. The CRM is a database that the contractor fills. Jobs inverts that model — it's a view into data that the contractor's purchasing activity has already generated. The contractor doesn't fill Jobs. Jobs fills itself.

This creates a fundamentally different cost structure. A CRM vendor's marginal cost per contractor is significant — servers, support, data storage, integrations, updates. QXO's marginal cost of organizing existing transaction data into a Job record is negligible. The data is already there. The infrastructure is already there. The only investment is the product experience that makes that data accessible and useful.

But free doesn't mean charity. Jobs is the strategic foundation of Ecom 2.0's retention model. A contractor who has six months of job history on QXO — margins tracked, proposals archived, addresses with full lifecycle data — faces a real switching cost if a competitor offers slightly cheaper shingles. That history doesn't transfer. The margin intelligence doesn't come with them. The homeowner records, the delivery confirmations, the trend data showing which neighborhoods and price points drive their best margins — all of that stays on QXO. Jobs is free because the value it creates for QXO in contractor retention and order consolidation vastly exceeds any subscription revenue it could generate.

The flywheel is explicit: better tools drive adoption. Adoption builds job history. Job history creates switching costs. Switching costs capture incremental orders. Incremental orders grow Customer Share of Wallet. Jobs is the mechanism that turns first-time ATB Blue users into long-term QXO-first contractors.

### How does Jobs deliver CRM functionality without being a CRM?

Jobs provides the capabilities that matter most to Tier 3 and Tier 2 contractors — the 80% of CRM value that drives daily operations — without the complexity, cost, or manual data entry of a standalone CRM.

A traditional CRM is a general-purpose relationship management tool. It tracks leads, contacts, opportunities, communications, tasks, and pipeline stages across any kind of business. It requires the user to define their workflow, configure their stages, enter their data, and maintain their records. For enterprise roofing companies with dedicated office staff, that flexibility is valuable. For a solo operator who does his own sales, installs, and bookkeeping, it's overhead he can't absorb.

Jobs replaces the CRM's most valuable outputs with automatic inputs. Proposal tracking — the CRM's pipeline view — is populated automatically every time the contractor sends a proposal through ATB Blue. Win/loss status updates when the homeowner accepts or declines. Order and fulfillment tracking updates as the supply chain moves. Margin analysis calculates itself from the proposal and invoice data. The contractor's "dashboard" isn't something they build and maintain — it's a real-time reflection of work they're already doing on the platform.

What Jobs doesn't do — and deliberately doesn't try to do in V1 — is replicate the CRM features that require data QXO doesn't have. Customer contact management beyond the address level, email and call logging, calendar integration, crew scheduling, and marketing automation are all CRM features that depend on information the contractor would need to manually enter. Those capabilities are on the roadmap as the platform matures, and Jobs is designed as the foundation container where they'll eventually live. But V1 focuses ruthlessly on the value that can be delivered automatically from supply chain data, because that's what makes Jobs fundamentally different from a CRM — it works without asking the contractor to do anything extra.

The cost comparison is stark. A contractor paying $200-500 per month for AccuLynx, ServiceTitan, or JobNimbus gets a powerful tool — but one that requires manual data entry and doesn't connect to their supplier. Jobs gives that contractor project tracking, proposal management, order history, delivery visibility, and margin analysis for free, populated by the purchasing they're already doing. For the majority of QXO's customer base, that's not a compromise. That's enough.

### How does Jobs support multi-trade and cross-LOB work?

A Job is anchored to an address, not to a single trade or line of business. This means one property can hold multiple scopes of work — roofing, siding, windows, gutters, commercial — each as a self-contained section within the same Job.

Think of the address as the orchestra. Each trade scope is a section — strings, brass, woodwinds. Each individual order or estimate within a scope is a chair within that section. The contractor is the conductor, managing the full picture at the property level while each section maintains its own integrity — its own materials, its own margin, its own delivery timeline.

When Marcus completes a roofing job at 445 Spruce Way and the homeowner asks about siding three months later, he doesn't start from scratch. He opens the existing Job for that address, adds a siding scope, and begins a new estimate within the same record. The roofing history — what was done, what was spent, what margin was realized — is still there. The siding scope has its own estimate, its own proposal, its own order, and its own margin tracking. But the address-level view shows both trades together, giving Marcus a complete picture of his relationship with that property.

This design has direct implications for reporting. A contractor can view margin and performance data at multiple levels: by individual order, by trade scope within a Job, by the full Job across all trades, or across all Jobs at a portfolio level. A contractor who does both roofing and siding can see whether their roofing margins are stronger than their siding margins, whether certain neighborhoods are more profitable for one trade versus another, and where their total materials spend is concentrating.

Multi-trade support also deepens the switching cost. A contractor whose QXO account contains job records spanning roofing and siding across dozens of addresses has built a business intelligence layer that doesn't exist anywhere else. No standalone CRM has that data because no standalone CRM is connected to the supply chain. The more trades a contractor runs through QXO, the more comprehensive their Job history becomes, and the harder it is to replicate that intelligence on another platform.

### How does Jobs help contractors grow their business?

Jobs turns every completed project into business intelligence that most contractors have never had access to.

A solo operator like Marcus can look at his Jobs dashboard and see patterns: his average margin on architectural shingle jobs is 36%, but it drops to 28% on designer shingle projects because he's not adjusting his labor markup enough to cover the slower installation pace. His proposals in the 80126 zip code have a 45% acceptance rate, but proposals in 80215 are closing at only 18% — suggesting he's pricing too high for that market or competing against more contractors there. His average time from proposal sent to homeowner acceptance is 3.2 days, but proposals that aren't accepted within 7 days almost never close.

None of these insights require an analytics add-on or a premium reporting tier. They emerge naturally from the data that accumulates inside Jobs — proposal outcomes, order costs, realized margins, and acceptance timelines. The platform surfaces this information because it's all already there.

For contractors looking to grow from solo operations into small crews, Jobs provides the operational visibility that makes scaling less risky. A contractor who is considering hiring a second crew can look at their Job history and see: how many leads are they generating per month? What's their close rate? How much revenue can they handle before job quality or margins start declining? These are questions that previously required a CRM, a bookkeeper, and a quarterly review to answer. Jobs answers them in real time from the contractor's own transaction data.

The growth path also serves QXO's interests directly. A contractor who grows from 8 jobs a month to 15 jobs a month — because Jobs gave them the visibility to scale confidently — is a contractor whose total materials spend through QXO grows proportionally. Jobs doesn't just retain existing spend. It enables the contractor growth that creates new spend.

### What does Jobs NOT do yet — and where is it going?

V1 of Jobs focuses on what can be delivered automatically from QXO's supply chain data. This means the initial release deliberately excludes capabilities that would require the contractor to manually input data that QXO doesn't already have.

Jobs V1 does not include lead management — capturing incoming leads from phone calls, web forms, or referrals, and tracking them through a pipeline before they become estimates. That requires data entry the contractor would need to do themselves, and V1 prioritizes value that's automatic.

Jobs V1 does not include crew scheduling or job calendar management. Assigning crew members to job dates and managing installation timelines requires workforce data that lives outside QXO's transaction systems.

Jobs V1 does not include direct QuickBooks, Xero, or accounting software integration. Syncing invoices and payments to the contractor's books is on the roadmap but depends on third-party integration work.

Jobs V1 does not include homeowner communication tools — email templates, follow-up sequences, or automated reminders for pending proposals. These are CRM-adjacent features that will be evaluated for future versions.

Jobs V1 does not include storm and insurance job workflows, which involve a different data model (adjuster reports, insurance scopes, supplements) that requires dedicated design.

Each of these capabilities is designed to fit inside the Jobs architecture when it's ready. The Job entity is the container. V1 populates that container with supply chain data. Future versions layer in contractor-entered data — leads, schedules, communications, accounting — that progressively move Jobs from project tracking toward a full business management platform.

The deliberate sequencing matters: deliver automatic value first, earn the contractor's trust and daily usage, then introduce manual-entry features once the platform is already indispensable. If Jobs launched with a full CRM feature set that required hours of setup and data migration, adoption would stall. By launching with a zero-configuration experience that works the moment a contractor places an order, Jobs builds the habit that makes future features feel like natural extensions rather than new demands.

### How does Jobs create a competitive moat for QXO?

Jobs is not a feature that competitors can copy by building an equivalent interface. It's a structural advantage that requires a specific type of data that only a distributor possesses.

A standalone CRM — AccuLynx, JobNimbus, ServiceTitan — can track a contractor's pipeline, manage their leads, schedule their crews, and send their invoices. What it cannot do is automatically populate project records with real-time materials order data, delivery tracking, proof-of-delivery photos, branch inventory availability, and realized margin calculations derived from actual purchase prices. That data belongs to the distributor. A CRM vendor would need every distributor the contractor uses to provide a live data feed — which no distributor has an incentive to provide, because it commoditizes their relationship with the contractor.

SRS Distribution's RoofHub is the closest competitive analog, and it's a strong product. But RoofHub is order-centric — each transaction exists independently, and contractors export proposals one-way into third-party CRMs to manage the lifecycle. RoofHub does not have a native Job entity. Building one would require rearchitecting their data model to connect transactions across time at the address level rather than treating each order as a standalone event. That's not a UI change — it's a fundamental data architecture decision that affects how every system in their stack relates to every other system.

QXO's advantage is that Jobs is being designed from the ground up as the organizing principle of Ecom 2.0, not retrofitted onto an existing order management system. Every system in the stack — estimation, proposals, ordering, fulfillment, delivery, invoicing — is built to write to the Job entity. The address is the primary key. The Job is the container. Everything connects through it.

The moat deepens with usage. Every completed Job adds to the contractor's historical intelligence — margin trends, acceptance rates, seasonal patterns, trade-level profitability, zip-code performance. That intelligence is derived from QXO's proprietary transaction data and cannot be exported to a competitor's platform. A contractor who has built 18 months of Job history on QXO would be walking away from a business intelligence asset that took 18 months of real purchasing activity to create. No sign-up bonus from a competing distributor replaces that.

This is the endgame of the CSoW flywheel. ATB Blue creates the first transaction. Builds makes the next transaction faster. Jobs makes every transaction part of an accumulating intelligence layer that gets more valuable the longer the contractor stays. Each layer adds switching cost. Together, they make QXO the path of least resistance for a contractor's entire materials spend.
