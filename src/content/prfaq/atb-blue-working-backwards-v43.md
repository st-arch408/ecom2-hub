**New Address-Based Tool Lets Contractors Generate Professional Multi-Tier Proposals in Minutes, Then Handles Everything When the Homeowner Says Yes**

**Seattle, WA — September 1, 2026** — Marcus Rivera was sitting in his truck outside a split-level ranch in Arvada, Colorado when his phone buzzed. A homeowner across town had found his business card and wanted a quote on a full tear-off and replace. Six months ago, Marcus would have scribbled the address on a receipt, driven to the property after his current job wrapped, climbed on the roof with a tape measure, driven home, spent three hours that evening running calculations and building a quote in a spreadsheet, and emailed it the next morning — hoping the homeowner hadn't already signed with someone faster.

Instead, Marcus pulled up QXO's Address to Bid Calculator on his phone, typed in the address, and paid $20 for an instant roof measurement report. Three pre-built proposal options appeared — a budget-conscious option using three-tab shingles, a standard package with architectural shingles and a 25-year warranty, and a premium tier with designer shingles and a 50-year warranty — each automatically configured from Marcus's preferred brands and purchasing priorities: the brands he trusts, the warranty certifications his customers expect, and the price points that match how he sells. He adjusted the labor markup on the premium tier by two percent, hit send, and a polished proposal landed in the homeowner's inbox before Marcus finished his lunch.

"I used to lose two or three jobs a month just because I wasn't fast enough," Marcus said. "Not because my price was wrong or my work wasn't good. I'd show up with a quote two days later and the homeowner would say they already signed with somebody. Now I'm sending proposals the same hour I get the call. He represents the largest segment of QXO's residential roofing customer base — roughly 80% — classified as Tier 3: independent contractors and small crews who handle everything themselves. These contractors don't have dedicated estimators. They can't afford $300-500 per month for enterprise software. And they lose work not because they lack skill or competitive pricing, but because they can't match the response speed of companies with office staff.

QXO's Address to Bid Calculator eliminates the bottleneck. Contractors enter a street address, pay $20, and the system retrieves a professional Bid Perfect roof measurement report instantly. The platform's intelligence layer — called Builds — recognizes how each contractor prefers to work. A premium-focused contractor targeting high-end neighborhoods sees different default packages than a value-oriented crew running production volume. The homeowner experience is equally simple. They receive a clean, professional proposal comparing three options side by side. They can review it on their own time or walk through it with the contractor. When they're ready, they select a tier, e-sign the proposal, and submit their deposit — all through the platform.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why is $20 the right price point for QXO?

Twenty dollars is not a revenue play on the report itself. It is the entry fee for a materials sales funnel.

When a contractor pays $20, QXO passes that cost through to EagleView for a Bid Perfect measurement report. There is no meaningful margin on the report sale for QXO. The entire $20 is customer acquisition cost.

The economics change the moment a homeowner signs the contract. That signature triggers a guaranteed Premium upgrade at no additional charge to the contractor, and that upgrade guarantees a materials order. Every single accepted proposal results in a Premium upgrade and a materials purchase. There is no probability to model on the upgrade itself. The only variable is the contractor's close rate, which determines how many $20 reports get purchased across leads that do not convert.

Here is the math on a won deal. QXO absorbs the Premium upgrade at a weighted average cost of $25.58 per report based on the current EagleView contract. The contractor who now holds a full Premium takeoff with exact linear footages for every ridge, hip, valley, rake, and eave needs materials to complete the job. At QXO's online average order value of $4,500 and a 22% distributor gross margin, that materials order generates approximately $990 in gross margin. The $25.58 upgrade cost yields a 39x return. For context, typical SaaS customer acquisition costs take 12 to 18 months to recover. ATB Blue's upgrade cost pays back the moment the order is placed.

Now zoom out to the full funnel. At a 30% close rate, a contractor purchases roughly 3.3 Bid Perfect reports at $20 each before winning a deal. That is about $67 in total measurement cost per won job. QXO's total cost on the won deal is $67 in Bid Perfect passthroughs plus $25.58 for the Premium upgrade, totaling roughly $93. Against $990 in material gross margin, the all-in CAC is still under 10% of gross margin with immediate payback.

Twenty dollars also works as a round number that is easy to market, easy to remember, and low enough to be invisible on a $15,000 roofing job. It is 0.33% of a contractor's gross profit at 40% margins. No contractor is going to lose sleep over $20 on a lead they believe in.

**Why not free for everyone?** A $20 entry cost serves as a natural quality filter. A contractor who believes in a lead will spend $20 without hesitation. A contractor who is not sure will skip it. This self-selection means QXO's Premium upgrade investment (the $25.58 per accepted deal) concentrates on leads that contractors have already pre-qualified with their own money. A free model removes this filter and exposes QXO to report costs on speculative or low-intent leads where the contractor has no skin in the game.

That said, there is a strong case for rewarding high-performing contractors. A contractor with a consistently high close rate and strong basket sizes generates enough materials margin to more than cover the cost of their reports, including the ones that do not convert. Rather than simply making reports free (which removes the quality filter entirely), the preferred model is to charge $20 upfront and credit that amount back to the contractor's QXO Wallet when qualifying criteria are met. The criteria could include a minimum close rate, a minimum order size, or a full-roof takeoff requirement. This preserves the skin-in-the-game signal on every report pull while effectively making reports free for contractors who are already generating strong materials revenue for QXO. Credits flowing into QXO Wallet also reinforce that platform as the contractor's central payment and rewards hub. The specific qualifying thresholds and rebate mechanics are explored in detail under Hot Debate Topic #7 below.

The bottom line: ATB Blue at $20 is not a measurement product with a margin. It is a materials sales funnel with a $20 qualification fee. The report fee is the loss leader. The roofing materials are the business.

### How does the two-tiered measurement model work?

ATB Blue uses a two-stage measurement approach that mirrors how experienced roofing contractors already operate: estimate big for the bid, refine precisely for the order.

**Stage 1 — Bid Perfect (for quoting).** When the contractor pays $20 and enters an address, the system retrieves a Bid Perfect report. This provides the macro-level data a contractor needs to build a competitive bid: total roof squares, predominant pitch, roof complexity classification, and satellite imagery. This is the same data a contractor would get by driving to the property and eyeballing the roof from the ground — except it's instant, it's measured, and it's delivered to their phone. The contractor uses this data to generate the good-better-best proposal, applies their labor and material markup, and sends it to the homeowner.

**Stage 2 — Premium (for ordering).** When the homeowner accepts the proposal, QXO upgrades the Bid Perfect data to a Premium report at no additional cost. The Premium report provides exact linear footages for every ridge, hip, valley, rake, eave, and drip edge — the granular data needed to generate a precise materials order. Because the aerial imagery was already captured and processed during the Bid Perfect stage, the Premium upgrade is a data refinement, not a new measurement. It populates immediately.

The critical design principle is decoupled data routing. The Premium data does not overwrite the contractor's original estimate. The sold proposal — the price the homeowner agreed to — is locked as a historical record. The Premium data routes directly into the materials order builder as a separate data layer. The contractor knows exactly what they charged the homeowner and exactly what they need to order, and those are intentionally kept as two distinct records.

This two-tiered model is not new to the roofing industry. Platforms like AccuLynx and JobNimbus already manage this workflow with EagleView's product line. What ATB Blue changes is who absorbs the cost and when. In the legacy model, contractors pay for both reports — Bid Perfect and Premium — on every lead, win or lose. In ATB Blue's model, the contractor pays $20 for the bid data on every lead, and QXO absorbs the Premium cost only on won deals where the resulting materials order flows through QXO. This shifts the upgrade cost from a sales expense to a fulfillment investment.

### Does the measurement gap between Bid Perfect and Premium affect the contractor's margin?

No. In practice, the Premium report usually increases the contractor's realized profit margin rather than shrinking it.

When a contractor bids using Bid Perfect data, they apply a defensive waste factor to account for cuts in valleys, hips, and rakes. This is standard practice across the industry — contractors bid conservatively to protect their margin on an unknown roof geometry. A typical Bid Perfect estimate on a 30-square roof gets padded with 15-18% waste, meaning the contractor prices the job assuming roughly 34.5-35.4 squares of material.

When the Premium report arrives after acceptance, it provides exact waste calculations based on actual linear footages. In most cases, the algorithmic waste is closer to 11% — meaning the contractor actually needs roughly 33.3 squares. The contractor charged the homeowner for the padded amount but orders the precise amount. The difference — typically 1-2 squares at $90-120 per square wholesale — is pure additional profit.

The rare downside case: what if the Bid Perfect estimate underestimated the roof complexity, and the Premium report reveals the contractor needs more materials than they quoted? The margin buffer math is forgiving. A $15,000 roof at 40% gross margin gives the contractor roughly $6,000 in gross profit. The items most likely to be underestimated by Bid Perfect — ridge cap, starter strip, drip edge — are the cheapest line items on a materials order. If the Premium report reveals the contractor needs two extra boxes of ridge cap, the wholesale cost is $80-120. That drops gross margin from 40% to 39.2%. On a complex hip-and-valley roof where multiple accessories are underestimated, the cumulative variance might reach $200-400 — still only a 2-3 percentage point margin impact on a 40% job.

The field shingles — the expensive part of the bill of materials — are well-estimated by total squares even from Bid Perfect data. The Bid Perfect → Premium gap affects linear-footage-dependent accessories, not the primary material cost.

The critical point for labor pricing: labor is bid by the square (total roof area), which Bid Perfect captures accurately. The measurement gap between Bid Perfect and Premium does not affect the labor side of the equation at all. A contractor's labor markup is set on total squares, and total squares don't change between Bid Perfect and Premium. The only variance is in accessory materials, which are a small fraction of total job cost.

The $20 Bid Perfect model means the contractor saves the cost of a full Premium report on every lost lead. At a 30% close rate, 7 out of 10 leads don't convert — that's 7 Premium reports the contractor never pays for. The savings on lost leads ($224 saved per 10 bids at $32 per Premium report) vastly outweigh the rare instance where a won bid has a minor material cost underestimation.

### Why is end-to-end ordering a growth driver?

The most significant operational leap in modern roofing software is the transition from localized estimation to integrated eProcurement. Historically, after a sales representative won a bid, a production manager had to manually transpose the estimate into a material order, call or fax their local distributor branch, and hope the pricing matched their initial quote. This manual break in the operational chain was a primary source of margin erosion, data entry errors, and job site delays.

ATB Blue establishes a direct, bidirectional data conduit between the contractor's workflow and QXO's enterprise resource planning (ERP) system. This represents the shift toward eProcurement 2.0, allowing small and medium-sized businesses to leverage enterprise-grade supply chain logistics. The core functionalities include:

1. **Real-Time Live Pricing:** An ATB estimate utilizes a contractor's specific, negotiated pricing for materials in real-time. This completely eliminates the risk of proposing a price to a homeowner based on outdated material costs.

2. **Live Inventory Confidence:** Contractors can verify that the local branch has the specific shingle color or metal panel profile required in stock before committing to an installation date with the homeowner, preventing scheduling conflicts and backorder delays.

3. **One-Click Material Ordering:** Upon securing the customer's signature, the software automatically converts the finalized estimate — now powered by Premium measurement data — into a precise material purchase order. This order is digitally transmitted directly to the QXO POS without requiring a phone call, email, or manual double-entry.

4. **Delivery Tracking and Reconciliation:** The eProcurement process loops back to provide the contractor with real-time GPS tracking of the delivery truck. Once materials are dropped at the job site, proof-of-delivery photos taken by the driver and digital invoices are automatically pushed back into QXO Jobs.

### How do the good, better, best options work?

When a contractor sets up their account, the system learns their preferences: which brands they carry, what warranty levels they typically offer, their standard labor rates, and their target margins. These preferences reflect the contractor's purchasing priorities — whether they optimize for price consciousness, rebate maximization, warranty certification requirements, or a combination. From that profile, ATB Blue pre-builds three proposal tiers for every job — a budget-conscious option, a mid-range standard, and a premium package. These tiers auto-populate every time the contractor enters an address, using real-time QXO pricing from their assigned branch and the Bid Perfect measurement data for that roof.

Contractors can adjust anything — swap products, change quantities, modify markup. But the goal is that the system's understanding of their business is accurate enough that they rarely need to. This is where the Builds intelligence layer earns its weight. A Build is no longer just a single pre-configured material package. It's a tiered set representing a complete project at three price points: materials, warranty level, brand positioning, and associated pricing all calibrated to how that specific contractor sells.

The intelligence is in the defaults. A contractor who exclusively sells GAF sees GAF across all three tiers. A contractor who targets high-end neighborhoods sees tiers calibrated for that market. A value-oriented crew running production volume sees tiers that match their margin structure. The system learns from the contractor's purchase history and profile, and the accuracy of these defaults is one of ATB Blue's most critical success factors — which is why Build override rate is a key metric we track.

Before a proposal goes out, the contractor applies their labor pricing, product markups, and margin adjustments to each tier. These are pre-configured in the contractor's profile and auto-applied, but can be overridden per proposal. The contractor also has the option to apply item-level material markup — a per-SKU percentage increase on the wholesale cost of individual materials, hidden from the homeowner alongside the labor markup. This gives contractors a second margin lever beyond labor: they can mark up specific high-margin items or adjust material pricing strategy by tier without changing their overall labor rates. The homeowner sees the final all-in project price for each tier — not the material cost breakdown, not QXO's wholesale pricing, not the item-level markups. The contractor controls exactly what the homeowner sees, and the markup structure gives them a complete pricing strategy across the three tiers without revealing the underlying economics. These labor and markup controls are foundational to delivering a complete, signable proposal and will carry forward into the future CRM experience.

**BRD Dependency — Item Preferences and Brand Saving:** ATB Blue's tier defaults depend on the contractor having saved item preferences and preferred brands at the account level. This capability does not currently exist on QXO.com. The site does not support saving brand preferences, preferred SKUs, or default item selections to a contractor's profile. This must be built as a prerequisite to ATB Blue — without it, there is no intelligence layer to power the good-better-best defaults, and every proposal starts from scratch. This should be called out as a critical dependency in the BRD.

### How does the homeowner see the proposal?

The contractor chooses the delivery method — a branded PDF export or a shareable link. But the PDF is the simple path. The real product vision is the bidirectional proposal link.

**BRD Dependency — Bidirectional Proposal Link:** The end-to-end ATB Blue flow requires an interactive, hosted proposal experience — a mini-site or dedicated page where the homeowner can view the good-better-best tiers, compare options, e-sign the proposal, submit a deposit, and trigger the automated order. This capability does not currently exist on QXO.com. It would need to be designed and built to support the full ATB Blue value loop. Without it, the proposal is a static PDF, the homeowner acceptance happens offline, and the contractor must manually return to QXO to place the order — which collapses the automation and puts us back in V1 territory. This is the single most critical net-new build required for ATB Blue.

The proposal itself carries the contractor's business name, their pricing, and a professional format that competes with anything a large roofing company would produce. The contractor sets a suggested installation date on every proposal. This date serves two purposes: it gives the homeowner a concrete timeline, and it establishes a buffer for materials delivery. The system validates that the suggested date is far enough out to accommodate branch fulfillment and delivery logistics — if a contractor proposes a date that's too close to allow for materials procurement, the system flags it before the proposal goes out. This prevents the scenario where a homeowner signs, the order fires, and the delivery can't arrive in time for the scheduled work.

When a contractor sends a proposal, QXO guarantees the pricing and material availability for a defined window. The homeowner can review and compare options without the risk that prices shift between receiving the proposal and accepting it. The contractor can quote with confidence. This guarantee is a competitive advantage for QXO — it locks in the order at a moment when the contractor might otherwise shop pricing across suppliers. The guarantee window must be long enough to give the homeowner reasonable decision time, but short enough that QXO can honor both the pricing and the inventory commitment. The specifics of the guarantee window — duration, expiration behavior, branch-level inventory reservation, and what happens when the window closes — are active design decisions being finalized with leadership.

### What happens after the homeowner accepts?

When the homeowner selects a tier and e-signs the proposal, three things happen simultaneously.

First, the sold proposal is locked. The price the homeowner agreed to, the tier they selected, and the scope of work are preserved as an immutable record. This is the contractor's contract — it does not change regardless of what happens next in the measurement or ordering process.

Second, QXO upgrades the contractor's Bid Perfect data to a Premium report at no cost. The Premium report provides exact linear footages — ridge, hip, valley, rake, eave, and drip edge measurements — along with algorithmic waste calculations. Because the aerial imagery was already captured during the Bid Perfect stage, the upgrade populates immediately. The Premium data routes directly into the materials order builder as a separate data layer, never overwriting the sold estimate.

Third, the system generates a complete materials order on the contractor's existing QXO credit account using the Premium data, routes it to the appropriate branch for fulfillment, and schedules delivery. The contractor gets notified immediately — push notification, email, and text. They don't need to log back in, re-enter quantities, or call their branch. The order is already placed with precise quantities derived from exact measurements.

The homeowner pays the contractor directly for the full project price. That financial relationship is between the homeowner and the contractor. QXO is not in the middle. When the homeowner accepts, ATB Blue triggers the materials order on the contractor's existing QXO credit account, paying QXO the way they always have.

This is the moment where ATB Blue directly impacts CSoW. Every accepted proposal is a materials order that flows through QXO by default rather than a competing supplier. And because the order is generated from Premium data — precise quantities, correct accessories, validated waste factors — the contractor typically orders less material than they quoted the homeowner for, pocketing the difference as additional margin. The Premium upgrade doesn't just automate the order. It makes the order more profitable for the contractor.

### Who pays whom, and what does QXO actually earn?

The financial relationships in ATB Blue must be unambiguous because they define QXO's role and its limits.

The homeowner pays the contractor. The full project price, including materials, labor, overhead, and profit, goes directly from the homeowner to the contractor. QXO is not in the payment flow between the homeowner and the contractor. QXO does not take a percentage of the project price. QXO does not earn a transaction fee on the homeowner's payment. The contractor sets the price, the homeowner agrees to it, and the contractor collects it.

The contractor pays QXO for materials. When the homeowner accepts a proposal and the order fires, the materials are purchased on the contractor's existing QXO credit account at the contractor's negotiated pricing. This is the same commercial relationship the contractor already has with their QXO branch. ATB Blue does not change the pricing, the credit terms, or the payment structure. QXO earns distributor margin on the materials sale. That is the entire revenue model.

Everything the contractor adds on top of QXO's material cost belongs to the contractor. Labor markup, overhead percentage, profit margin, and item-level material markup are configured by the contractor in the proposal builder and applied to the homeowner-facing price. The homeowner sees a single all-in project price per tier. They do not see QXO's wholesale pricing, the contractor's labor rate, the overhead calculation, or any item-level markups. The contractor controls what the homeowner sees. QXO's wholesale cost is never exposed.

The item-level material markup deserves specific mention. In addition to labor and overhead, the contractor can apply a per-SKU percentage increase on the wholesale cost of individual materials, hidden from the homeowner alongside the labor markup. This gives contractors a second margin lever: they can mark up specific high-margin items or adjust material pricing strategy by tier without changing their overall labor rates. This capability exists because contractors routinely mark up materials beyond wholesale and expect any proposal tool to support that practice. QXO does not participate in or benefit from these markups. They flow entirely to the contractor.

ATB Blue is also designed to support third-party homeowner financing integrations. A contractor who wants to offer the homeowner a payment plan or financing option can do so through an integrated financing partner. This is not QXO lending. It is a third-party service that facilitates the transaction between the homeowner and the contractor. SRS Distribution has announced a planned integration with ProFund for this capability in RoofHub, establishing it as a competitive expectation. ATB Blue should support equivalent functionality at or near launch. The specific financing partner is a vendor selection decision, not a product design decision. The platform needs to support the integration point regardless of which provider is selected.

### What happens when a homeowner declines or doesn't respond?

This is where ATB Blue generates value even when the contractor doesn't win the job. When a homeowner declines a proposal — rather than simply letting it expire — the system captures a structured reason for the decline. Did the price feel too high? Were they going with a different contractor? Did they decide not to do the project at all? Were they waiting on insurance? Did they want a different material or brand?

This decline data is not just a log entry. It feeds back into the analytics layer that powers Builds and tier recommendations. If homeowners in a specific market consistently decline the premium tier as overpriced, the system can flag that signal to the contractor and suggest adjusting their markup or material selection for that segment. If a particular shingle brand is correlating with higher decline rates, that's intelligence QXO can surface to both the contractor and the manufacturer. Over time, this creates a feedback loop where every proposal — won or lost — makes the next proposal smarter.

For the contractor, this data translates into a conversion rate optimization tool. They can see which tiers are winning, which are being declined and why, and how their acceptance rates compare across neighborhoods, price points, and seasons. These analytics are value levers that deepen the contractor's dependency on ATB Blue — the tool doesn't just help them bid, it helps them bid better. This capability will become significantly more powerful when it rolls into the future CRM experience, where pipeline analytics and win/loss reporting provide a complete picture of the contractor's sales performance.

### How is ATB Blue fundamentally different from TC V1?

Takeoff Calculator V1 serves contractors who have already won a job and need to order materials. Those contractors have been calling branches and ordering materials successfully for years. V1 makes an existing process slightly more convenient, but it doesn't solve the most valuable problem that was preventing contractors from being successful. Pre-launch testing confirmed this: users didn't feel V1 provided enough standalone value.

ATB Blue solves a different and more acute problem — contractors losing jobs because they can't generate professional proposals fast enough. This isn't an incremental improvement to V1. It serves a different persona (the contractor in sales mode, not fulfillment mode), at a different moment in their workflow (before the job is won, not after), with a different value proposition (win more work, not just order materials faster). ATB Blue enters the workflow at the moment the order is *created*. The proposal itself is built on QXO pricing, QXO inventory, and QXO Builds.

### What is Customer Share of Wallet and why is it the foundational metric for ATB Blue?

Customer Share of Wallet (CSoW) measures the percentage of a contractor's total building materials spend that flows through QXO versus competitors. It's calculated as: QXO Spend / Total Estimated Annual Spend across all suppliers. A contractor spending $100K/year who gives QXO $90K has a 90% CSoW. A contractor spending $2M/year who gives QXO $400K has a 20% CSoW. The second generates more raw revenue, but the first represents a fundamentally deeper, more defensible customer relationship.

ATB Blue's success is not measured by how many proposals contractors create or how many homeowners accept them. Those are input metrics — they matter because they drive the output. The output that matters is whether contractors who use ATB Blue give QXO a larger share of their total materials spend over time than contractors who don't.

The hypothesis is direct: every proposal generated through ATB Blue is built on QXO pricing and converts into a QXO order upon acceptance. Every order that flows through ATB Blue is an order that might otherwise have gone to ABC Supply or SRS Distribution. Over time, the convenience of having proposals, orders, Build profiles, and pricing history all living on one platform creates switching costs that make consolidating spend at QXO the path of least resistance. This is the CSoW flywheel — better tools drive digital adoption, digital adoption builds switching costs, switching costs capture incremental orders, incremental orders grow wallet share.

The specific CSoW hypothesis for ATB Blue: compare the CSoW of calculator adopters versus non-adopters over 90 days post-launch. If adopters show measurable CSoW velocity (positive percentage-point change per quarter), the tool is working. If they don't, we've built something contractors use but that doesn't change where they spend.

### What are we NOT building — and what does ATB Blue open the door for?

ATB Blue is not a CRM. It does not include pipeline analytics, win/loss reporting, follow-up automation, or lead management in its initial scope. It does not include job scheduling, crew management, or project tracking beyond basic proposal status. It does not include QuickBooks or accounting integration. It does not include siding — that remains a separate workstream pending dedicated research. It does not include commercial roofing, which serves a different customer tier with enterprise requirements.

These boundaries are deliberate. Every feature we don't build keeps the tool fast, simple, and focused on the one thing that drives CSoW: getting a QXO-priced proposal in front of a homeowner faster than any competitor can.

However, ATB Blue is explicitly designed as the foundation on which the future CRM experience will be built. Every address entered in ATB Blue creates a Job — the atomic unit of project tracking on QXO. A Job is an address with proposals, orders, statuses, and history attached to it. Jobs are where pipeline analytics, win/loss reporting, follow-up automation, lead management, job scheduling, and crew management will eventually live. ATB Blue establishes the Job entity and the data model that powers it. When the CRM comes, it doesn't start from zero — it inherits a populated database of real Jobs with real proposal data, real acceptance rates, real decline reasons, and real order history. ATB Blue is the growth engine; the CRM is the management layer that sits on top of it.

### What are the biggest risks?

Five risks stand highest.

First, Builds accuracy at launch. The entire value proposition depends on the system's recommended tiers being close to what contractors would actually configure themselves. If the defaults miss the mark — wrong brands, wrong waste factors, wrong warranty levels — contractors will override everything manually, and the tool becomes a slow calculator instead of a fast proposal engine.

Second, measurement reliability. ATB Blue's speed advantage depends on instant aerial measurements being accurate enough that contractors trust them without climbing on the roof. The success of the measurement calculations of the takeoff calculator V1 is imperative to the success of this project.

Third, homeowner adoption of the digital acceptance flow. The e-signature and deposit submission through the platform is new behavior for homeowners who are accustomed to verbal agreements or paper contracts. If the digital acceptance creates friction — if homeowners don't trust the platform, don't understand the interface, or prefer to just call the contractor and say yes — then the automated order trigger doesn't fire and we lose the core value loop. Without digital acceptance, the proposal is just a PDF and the order still requires manual placement, which puts us back in the same competitive position as V1.

Fourth, the price guarantee and expiration mechanism. When a contractor sends a proposal with QXO-guaranteed pricing, we are making a commitment on both price and inventory for a defined window. If that window is too short, homeowners don't have enough time to decide and the proposal loses its professional edge. If it's too long, QXO carries pricing risk during material cost fluctuations and inventory reservation risk at the branch level. Getting this wrong in either direction undermines contractor confidence — either the homeowner feels rushed, or the contractor discovers that the pricing they quoted is no longer honored when the homeowner finally accepts. The expiration mechanism must be clear to all parties: the contractor, the homeowner, and the branch. It must communicate what happens when the window closes (does the proposal die, auto-update, or require regeneration?) and it must protect QXO's ability to honor commitments without absorbing unreasonable risk. This is an active design problem that directly impacts both the contractor experience and QXO's margin exposure.

Fifth, pricing adoption friction. The $20 per-report model is designed to be negligible relative to job value, but it is not free. Tier 3 contractors — the 80% of QXO's customer base that ATB Blue is built for — are cost-sensitive by nature. If these contractors perceive the $20 as "paying QXO to do my job" rather than "investing $20 to win a $15,000 job," adoption will stall. The framing matters: $20 buys a professional measurement report, a polished multi-tier proposal, and a free Premium upgrade if the homeowner says yes. That value proposition must be communicated clearly at the point of first use. If early adoption data shows that report purchase volume is lower than expected despite active accounts, the pricing model may need adjustment — whether that's a promotional free-tier onboarding period, volume incentives, or a revised price point. This is why the pricing model is being validated through contractor focus groups before it's committed to the product spec.

### What's the rollout strategy?

Denver metro first. Denver is where we have the strongest research foundation, the most validated Build templates, and the simplest onboarding experience. Regional measurement variations, building codes, and material preferences collapse when you constrain to one market. This lets us isolate the core question: does ATB Blue change contractor behavior in a way that moves CSoW?

The pilot validates four hypotheses. First, will contractors pay $20 per address to generate proposals through their materials distributor? Second, does the good-better-best tier model resonate with how they actually sell to homeowners? Third, does automated order conversion from accepted proposals create measurable wallet share displacement — do ATB Blue users consolidate more of their spend at QXO than non-users over the same period? Fourth, does the two-tiered measurement model (Bid Perfect for bidding, free Premium on acceptance for ordering) feel natural to contractors, or does the gap between estimate data and order data create friction or mistrust?

Post-pilot expansion follows branch geography. When contractors from Phoenix or Atlanta create accounts, the system applies region-specific defaults based on their branch's market data. National coverage builds organically through usage rather than upfront configuration. Each new market is a new data source that improves Builds accuracy for the next market.

### How will we measure success?

CSoW is the north star. Every other metric is an input that we believe drives it. But CSoW alone doesn't tell the full story — we also track total spend growth alongside wallet share. A contractor whose CSoW moves from 30% to 50% is valuable. A contractor whose CSoW moves from 30% to 50% *and* whose total QXO spend grows from $75K to $150K is transformative. We want both: deeper share and bigger absolute numbers.

Three additional metrics sit alongside CSoW as primary success indicators for ATB Blue:

**Proposal-to-Acceptance Rate** measures the relationship between how many proposals contractors generate and how many homeowners accept them. This is a two-sided metric. On the contractor side, a high proposal volume with a low acceptance rate suggests the proposals aren't compelling enough, the pricing is off, or the tiers don't match what homeowners in that market want. On the homeowner side, a healthy acceptance rate validates that the good-better-best format and the interactive proposal experience are working as intended. The relationship between these two numbers — proposal volume and acceptance rate — is the leading indicator of whether ATB Blue is helping contractors win work. If contractors are sending more proposals but winning at the same or higher rate, the tool is expanding their capacity without diluting their close rate. If acceptance rates drop as volume increases, the quality of the proposals is degrading and the Builds intelligence needs calibration.

**Total QXO Spend Growth (ATB Blue Users)** measures whether contractor spend at QXO is growing in absolute dollars, not just as a share of wallet. CSoW can increase because a contractor consolidates existing spend — which is valuable — but the most powerful outcome is when ATB Blue drives net-new spend. A contractor who wins two additional jobs per month because they can bid faster, and those orders flow through QXO, represents incremental revenue that didn't exist before. We will track the YoY spend trajectory of ATB Blue adopters versus non-adopters to isolate this effect.

**Homeowner Decline Rate with Reason** measures how often homeowners decline proposals and, critically, why. Every declined proposal with a captured reason — price too high, chose another contractor, project deferred, waiting on insurance, wanted different materials — is a data point that feeds back into the Builds intelligence and the contractor's own conversion analytics. Over time, the pattern of decline reasons across markets, price points, and seasons becomes a competitive advantage that no CRM can replicate because no CRM has visibility into both the proposal and the supply chain.

The input metrics that feed these outcomes are the operational dials we can tune. Address-to-Bid completion rate tells us whether contractors are finishing proposals or abandoning the flow. Report purchase volume tells us whether the $20 price point is calibrated correctly — if purchase volume is healthy but acceptance rates are low, the tool is reaching the right audience but the proposals need work; if purchase volume itself is low despite active accounts, the price or value proposition needs adjustment. Proposal send rate tells us whether completed proposals are reaching homeowners. Time to proposal tells us whether we're delivering on the speed promise — target is under 15 minutes from address entry to proposal sent. Build override rate tells us whether the intelligence layer is accurate — target is under 30%, and anything higher signals that our recommendations are missing how contractors actually buy.

Each of these inputs has a hypothesized causal link to CSoW. If contractors purchase reports and complete proposals and send them, and homeowners accept them, and those acceptances convert to QXO orders, and the Builds are accurate enough that contractors don't need to rebuild everything manually — then the cumulative effect should be measurable wallet share growth and total spend growth. If any link in that chain breaks, the input metrics will tell us where.

## Hot Debate Topics

### 1. Item Preferences and Brand Saving: Critical Prerequisite

ATB Blue's tier defaults depend on the contractor having saved item preferences and preferred brands at the account level. This capability does not currently exist on QXO.com. The site does not support saving brand preferences, preferred SKUs, or default item selections to a contractor's profile.

Without this, there is no intelligence layer to power the good-better-best defaults, and every proposal starts from scratch, making ATB Blue a slower, more manual experience than RoofHub's template system. This is the highest-priority prerequisite. The question for engineering is not whether to build it, but how deep the preference model needs to be at launch. Minimum viable: brand preference and warranty level per product category. Full vision: per-SKU defaults with learned weighting from purchase history.

### 2. Bidirectional Proposal Link: The Most Critical Net-New Build

The end-to-end ATB Blue value loop requires an interactive, hosted proposal experience. A page where the homeowner can view tiers, compare options, e-sign, and submit a deposit. This does not exist on QXO.com today.

Without it, the proposal is a static PDF, the homeowner acceptance happens offline (phone call or email), and the contractor must manually return to QXO to trigger the order. This collapses the automation and eliminates the primary differentiation against both CRMs and RoofHub. The bidirectional proposal link is the single feature that makes ATB Blue's acceptance-to-order path automatic rather than manual. If we ship ATB Blue without it, we have built a slightly better version of V1.

The open design questions: How is the link structured (subdomain, vanity URL, unique hash)? What happens when the proposal expires? Does the homeowner create an account or is this a guest experience? How do we handle multiple proposals to the same homeowner from different contractors?

### 3. Price Guarantee Window and Expiration

When a contractor sends a proposal, QXO guarantees the pricing and material availability for a defined window. The duration of this window is not finalized.

Too short (7 days): homeowners feel rushed, acceptance rates drop, and the professional edge of the proposal is undermined. Too long (60+ days): QXO carries pricing risk during material cost fluctuations, and branch-level inventory reservations become operationally complex.

The expiration mechanism needs to answer: Does the proposal die when the window closes, auto-update with new pricing, or require the contractor to regenerate? Does QXO reserve inventory at the branch level during the guarantee period, or is availability checked at the moment of acceptance? If prices increase during the window, does QXO absorb the difference or pass it through?

This directly impacts QXO's margin exposure and the contractor's confidence in the tool. It needs a decision from both product and finance leadership.

### 4. SRS RoofHub Velocity and Feature Parity Risk

SRS's digital engineering team of approximately 150 engineers is shipping 3 to 5 AI capabilities per month. Their August 2025 release introduced AI-generated options in Smart Templates, a direct move toward the auto-configuration that ATB Blue positions as a differentiator. Their planned but unshipped roadmap includes ProFund financing, photo uploads with annotations, enriched property reports, and a proposal analytics dashboard.

The strategic risk is not that RoofHub has more features today. It does, and it has for years. The risk is that ATB Blue's differentiation points (measurement economics, auto-generated tiers, automated acceptance-to-order) have a shelf life. If SRS ships auto-tier generation and absorbs measurement costs on won deals within the next 12 months, ATB Blue's competitive narrative narrows significantly.

The question for the organization: What is ATB Blue's time-to-market, and does that timeline account for the probability that SRS closes specific feature gaps before we launch? If ATB Blue ships Q3 2026 and SRS ships auto-tier generation and financing by Q2 2026, the competitive landscape on launch day may look different than the one described in this document.

### 5. Measurement Data Accuracy: Inherited Risk from TC V1

ATB Blue's speed advantage depends on Bid Perfect measurements being accurate enough that contractors trust them without climbing on the roof. The success of ATB Blue is downstream of the measurement calculation accuracy established in Takeoff Calculator V1.

If V1's measurement calculations have known accuracy issues, even edge cases on complex roof geometries, those issues carry forward into ATB Blue proposals. A contractor who generates a proposal based on inaccurate Bid Perfect data and loses money on the Premium order will not use the tool again. The first few incorrect proposals in the Denver pilot could poison adoption through contractor word-of-mouth. These accuracy questions will be validated through the internal pilot prototype launch of V1.

### 6. Financial Relationship Clarity: QXO's Role Boundaries

ATB Blue creates a three-party transaction: the homeowner pays the contractor, the contractor pays QXO for materials, and QXO pays EagleView for measurement data. QXO's revenue is exclusively the distributor margin on materials. All labor markup, overhead, profit margin, and item-level material markup flow to the contractor.

This must be communicated clearly to both internal stakeholders and contractors. Internally, ATB Blue's revenue attribution should be measured as materials margin driven by the tool, not as report fee revenue. The $20 per report is a cost recovery mechanism, not a revenue line. If internal reporting treats report fees as product revenue, it will misrepresent ATB Blue's business model and create misaligned incentive structures.

Externally, contractors need to understand that QXO does not see, track, or benefit from the labor and markup they apply to proposals. The contractor's pricing strategy is private. This is a trust prerequisite. If contractors suspect QXO is using their markup data to adjust wholesale pricing or target them with competitor products, adoption will stall.

### 7. Loyalty Rebate Model: How to Reward High-Performing Contractors Without Removing the Quality Filter

The $20 Bid Perfect fee works as customer acquisition cost and as a lead quality filter. But the 39x return on won deals means QXO is leaving significant goodwill on the table by charging contractors who consistently close and order materials. The question is how to give it back without creating a free-for-all that attracts report abuse or competitor leakage.

The core principle across all options: charge $20 upfront on every report pull, then credit it back to QXO Wallet when the contractor meets qualifying criteria. This preserves the skin-in-the-game signal while effectively making reports free for contractors who generate strong materials revenue. Credits flowing into QXO Wallet reinforce that platform as the contractor's central financial hub across all QXO products.

There is also a risk that needs to be addressed in any model: a contractor could pull free (or effectively free) Bid Perfect reports through QXO and use those measurements to order materials from a competitor. The rebate mechanics below are designed to tie the credit directly to QXO purchasing behavior, not just to proposal activity.

**Option A: Full-Roof Order Credit.** The simplest version. The contractor pays $20 per report. If the homeowner accepts and the resulting materials order is a full roof replacement (not a partial repair), QXO credits the $20 back to QXO Wallet. If the deal does not close or the order is a partial repair below a defined threshold, the $20 stays collected.

This directly addresses the partial repair concern. A contractor pulling a Bid Perfect for a $600 ridge cap repair does not get a credit. A contractor closing a $4,500+ full replacement does. The downside is binary thinking: the contractor either gets the full credit or nothing, which does not reward incremental improvement in close rates or basket sizes.

**Option B: Basket Threshold Tiers.** The $20 credit scales with order size. Orders over $2,500 earn the $20 credit. Orders over $5,000 earn $20 plus an additional bonus credit (for example, $10 or $15 toward the next report). Orders under $2,500 earn nothing.

This creates a natural incentive to push homeowners toward higher tiers and larger basket sizes. It also makes the math work cleanly: at 22% GM, a $2,500 order generates $550 in margin, which easily covers the $20 credit plus the $25.58 upgrade cost. The tiered structure gives contractors something to aim for and makes "upgrade to the Better tier" a financially rewarded behavior.

**Option C: QXO Wallet Spending Account (Preferred in Combination with B).** Instead of crediting $20 per qualifying order, QXO deposits a percentage of each qualifying materials order into QXO Wallet as a running balance. For example, 1% of every order over $2,500. On a $4,500 order, that is $45 in Wallet credit, more than enough to cover two future Bid Perfect reports.

This model creates a self-funding cycle. The more a contractor closes and orders, the larger their Wallet balance grows, and the more "free" reports they can pull from that balance. Contractors who go cold or take their business to a competitor see their earning rate drop and their balance stop growing. The Wallet balance also becomes a switching cost: a contractor sitting on $200 in QXO Wallet credits has a reason to keep pulling reports through QXO rather than moving to RoofHub.

The combination of B and C is particularly strong. B sets the qualifying floor (only orders above $2,500 earn credits). C determines the reward mechanic (percentage-based deposit into Wallet rather than a flat $20 rebate). Together they create a system where high-performing contractors accumulate Wallet value proportional to their materials purchasing, which they can spend on future Bid Perfect reports or potentially other QXO services.

**Option D: Confidence Score Multiplier (Bronze / Silver / Gold).** QXO builds an internal contractor scoring system based on trailing 90-day close rate, average basket size, order frequency, and digital engagement (percentage of orders placed through QXO.com or integrations). Each contractor receives a tier assignment that determines their Wallet earning rate:

Bronze (baseline): 0.5% of qualifying orders deposited to Wallet. This is the default tier for new or low-activity contractors. At $4,500 AOV, that is $22.50 per won deal, barely covering one report. Bronze contractors are essentially paying full price with a small loyalty perk.

Silver (mid-tier): 1.0% of qualifying orders. At $4,500 AOV, $45 per won deal. A Silver contractor closing 3 deals per month accumulates $135/month in Wallet credit, covering roughly 7 Bid Perfect reports. At that volume, reports are effectively free if they maintain their close rate.

Gold (top performers): 1.5% of qualifying orders plus priority access to new features and beta programs. At $4,500 AOV, $67.50 per won deal. A Gold contractor at 15+ reports per month with a 35%+ close rate is generating so much materials margin that the Wallet credits are a rounding error on QXO's P&L, but they represent significant perceived value to the contractor.

The scoring system also creates a natural defense against competitor leakage. If a contractor's close rate drops because they are sending some deals through a competitor, their score drops and their earning rate falls. The system rewards loyalty without explicitly policing behavior.

The open question on Option D is complexity. Building a scoring engine, surfacing tier status to contractors, managing tier transitions, and communicating the rules all require product and engineering investment that may not be justified at launch. This could be a Phase 2 enhancement layered on top of a simpler Option A or B+C launch model.

**Option E: Closing Bonus.** Rather than crediting back the $20 report cost, QXO deposits a flat bonus (for example, $50) into QXO Wallet for every qualified close that meets minimum criteria (full roof replacement, order placed through QXO, minimum basket size of $2,500). The $20 report fee is not refunded. Instead, the contractor receives a larger reward that more than covers the report cost and incentivizes the behavior QXO actually cares about: closing deals and ordering materials.

At $50 per qualified close, a contractor with a 30% close rate pulling 10 reports per month wins 3 deals and earns $150 in Wallet credits against $200 in report costs. They are still paying net $50/month for reports, but the closing bonus reframes the relationship from "I pay for reports" to "I earn rewards when I close." At a 40% close rate, the same contractor wins 4 deals, earns $200, and breaks even on report costs entirely.

The psychology matters here. A closing bonus feels like QXO is investing in the contractor's success rather than refunding a fee. It also decouples the reward from the report cost, which gives QXO flexibility to adjust the $20 price point or the $50 bonus independently over time.

**Comparison of all five options:**

| Dimension | A: Full-Roof Credit | B: Basket Tiers | C: Wallet % | D: Score Multiplier | E: Closing Bonus |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reward per won deal ($4,500 AOV) | $20 credit | $20 to $35 credit | ~$45 (at 1%) | $22.50 to $67.50 | $50 flat |
| Partial repair protection | Yes (binary) | Yes (threshold) | Yes (threshold) | Yes (scoring) | Yes (criteria) |
| Competitor leakage defense | Low | Low | Medium (balance as switching cost) | High (score drops) | Medium (tied to QXO orders) |
| QXO Wallet reinforcement | Basic | Basic | Strong | Strong | Strong |
| Implementation complexity | Low | Low | Medium | High | Low |
| Contractor perception | "Fee refund" | "Tiered rewards" | "Earning account" | "Loyalty program" | "Performance bonus" |
| Recommended phase | Launch MVP | Launch MVP | Launch or Phase 2 | Phase 2 | Launch alternative |

**Recommended approach for launch:** Start with Option B (basket threshold tiers) as the minimum viable mechanic, with Option C (Wallet percentage) layered on if the QXO Wallet platform is ready. This combination is simple enough to ship at launch, addresses the partial repair risk, creates a Wallet switching cost, and scales naturally with contractor performance. Option D (confidence scoring) should be planned as a Phase 2 enhancement once QXO has enough behavioral data to build meaningful scores. Option E is an alternative to B if the team prefers a "bonus" framing over a "credit" framing.

The qualifying criteria across all options should use trailing 90-day data to prevent gaming and to reward sustained performance rather than one-off spikes. A volume floor of 5 reports per month should also apply to prevent low-volume contractors from qualifying on a single lucky close.
